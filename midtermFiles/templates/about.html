Data Limitations

Despite the high quality and granular nature of this data set, it nonetheless has a number of limitations. Most importantly, it is important to stress that our data only assesses the flow of aid from OECD member nations to non-OECD nations. Therefore, there are a few notably missing nations, such as China and Russia, because they are not OECD member nations. However, it is well known that both of these nations have been increasing active in the foreign aid scene, primarily focused on infrastructure projects. This focus is part of their foreign policy strategy to strengthen their soft power within developing countries and reap economic boons later once the economies of these developing nations stabilize.

In addition to omitting important world powers that are nonetheless not member nations in the OECD data also lacks information on foreign aid passed between developing countries. Often, nations will pass foreign aid to other nations in similar economic positions as both a show of good faith and a political tool for positioning a favorable economic policy. For instance, our research indicated many African and Asian nations, the two continents that drawn in the greatest quantities of foreign aid, also donate between one another to further economic pursuits and stability along their respective borders.

Finally, while not immediately the focus of our project, it may have been beneficial to differentiate between the types of aid provided from OECD countries to non-OECD nations. For example, foreign investments in infrastructure, medical services, and food security are immensely important for developing nations as they ensure the health of their citizens and support a healthy and efficient workforce to help build a thriving economy. However, it is likely that a significant portion of the aid provided, and particularly the aid from the United States, is in the form of military support and resources. These types of aid, while perhaps useful in the short-term, do little to support the long-term stability and prosperity of developing nations. Therefore, our visualizations may be indicating a disproportionate American influence due to its expansive Military Industrial Complex since while these resources are indeed expansive, they may not be as beneficial as other non-violent related forms of foreign aid.

User Testing

During the early stages of our analysis, we consulted with a convenience sample of individuals likely representative of the types of users who would find these applications helpful. Initially, we used simple line plots to show the total amount of foreign aid both given and received over time. In response to these design choices, users expressed slight dissatisfaction at not being able to dig into the data from a temporal aspect. As this point, we modified our design to enable users to specify the time period of interest. Similarly, many of our test users stated a desire to filter by OECD nation as to better understand the specific flows between each donating and receiving nation. In response, we both included a filter option and converted our line plots to a more intuitive Sankey diagram which excels at communicating flows between two endpoints.

We undertook a similar process when designing our first dashboard. In the early stages, we familiarized ourselves with the data set to understand how best to represent the differing components in an interactive and intuitive format. Following this exploratory data analysis, we brainstormed and sketched our prototype on paper to form a preliminary design. Then, we finally transferred these designs into Tableau on a dashboard and reached out to our convenience sample for feedback. They expressed excitement and interest in the design and subject matter but were frustrated that they could not intuit how to use the initial clunky and somewhat obscure layout. As a result, we simplified our original design and streamlined the interactivity to ensure that the different panels of the dashboard were interconnected. This ensured that when users began filtering with one component of the design, the others would adjust accordingly. We also deleted the legend for recipient countries since it did not add any new and relevant information. Similarly, we separated the continent legends in two different titles in order to display additional information.

Throughout the user-testing experience, we gained incredible insight on how users might interact with our interactive visualizations and which factors are most important for different types of users. These personalized experience allowed us to incorporate more relevant pieces of information into our design while minimizing extraneous details that the users did not deem as critical to the overall presentation of the design. Furthermore, we realized how critical intuition is in our interactive dashboards. Without instructions, users easily become frustrated and become disengaged with the design, and more broadly with the entire content of our web application. Conversely, after we adapted our dashboards to incorporate our usersâ€™ feedback and reintroduced these products to the test users, they immediately became engrossed in the content of our website and enjoyed learning about the nuanced ebbs and flows of foreign aid.
