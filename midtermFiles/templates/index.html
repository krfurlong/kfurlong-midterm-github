OECD Foreign Aid from 1960-2016

A project for Interactive Data Science at Carnegie Mellon University by Lina Osorio and Kyle Furlong

Introduction

Foreign aid plays an incredibly important role in the economies of developing nations and in ensuring the international security of developed nations worldwide. Largely viewed in the academic and political communities as invaluable to global stability, foreign aid from wealthy nations to less politically and/or economically stable nations helps connect different peoples and lessens the prevalence and severity of global conflict and terrorism. Even current generals in the United States recognize the importance of foreign aid as provided through the U.S. Department of State, going as far to state:

“If you don't fund the State Department fully, then I need to buy more ammunition.”

James Mattis, U.S. Secretary of Defense (2016-)

Alex Radelich

Nitish Meena

Given these widely acknowledged social benefits to international foreign aid, the increasingly isolationist rhetoric of the Trump administration comes as a significant threat to global security. While funding national defense efforts is indeed of great importance to the safety of the United States and its citizens, both domestic and abroad, funding peace efforts in areas of conflict within developing nations actually is more effective as it stems conflict and crisis at its source. However, historically, the extent to which the developed world provided aid to the developing world has been somewhat limited and unfortunately, often reserved for countries or regions at the onset of conflict.

To this end, we pose the following question:


How has foreign aid from OECD nations changed over time? Moreover, how has this international community responded with foreign aid during times of crisis and conflict from 1960 to 2016?

The Data

The Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) is an international economic organization comprised of thirty-five member nations. Founded in 1961, the stated purpose of this critical intergovernmental group is to promote policies that will improve the economic and social well-being of people around the world. by encouraging global trade and international economic outreach activities. As a part of its pursuit of this missin, the OECD has collected vast amounts of data on the economic activities of the world and in particular those of the member nations. The topics of their database range from health indicators to measures of social/technical innovation to economic activities; however, for the purpose of our analysis, we pulled publically available data regarding the quantity of foreign aid from OECD member nations to non-OECD nations from the inception of this organization (1960) to the most recent year available (2016).

While incredibly comprehensive, the initial structure of our data was not ideal for web visualization with Tableau’s resources. For this reason, we imported our data set into R for some minor data cleaning and significant data wrangling to transform the data from a “wide” format ideal for time-series cluster analysis to a “long” format ideal for visualization. Furthermore, our data lacked any indication of the continent of each donating and receiving country, so we joined this information to our data set using SQL statements. Finally, due to the sheer size of our data set and the computationally taxing nature of big data web visualization in Tableau, we chose to filter our data using SQL statements to only include information where an OECD nation donated at least $500,000 to a non-OECD nation, adjusted for inflation. Although this intentional omission results in slightly less detailed visualizations, the overall narrative remains the same and the omitted data can be easily downloaded for those interested in this granular data.

Mapping Foreign Aid, 1960-2016

With our data well-defined and other potential data limitations considered, we can begin our analysis. The following dashboard shows the worldwide relationship between the donor nation and recipient of foreign aid from 1960 to 2016. To see a more detailed analysis, we can drill down the information presented to evaluate the flow of aid on both the continent and the country level. As you can see clearly in the continents legend in the top-right corner of the dashboard, historically most of the aid goes to Africa (blue square) and Asia (orange square), and the primary donor is Europe (red square) as seen in the data summary on the right-side of the dashboard.

In using the visualization to view foreign aid by country as a function of time, we noted that donations from European nations spike after 2000 as a result of the EU fight against poverty as part of the Millennium Development Goals (MDG) and the impending War Against Terrorism immediately following 9/11. We invite you explore the data in more detail by clicking on any of the countries in the map to zoom in and filter the aid distributed by donor for that region. Understanding Trends in Foreign Aid, 1960-2016

While our earlier dashboard is incredibly helpful in understanding aggregate trends of foreign aid, it is also important to understand foreign aid in terms of the specific flow of funds between developed and developing nations. To do so, we designed the following graph, a Sankey diagram, which shows the flow of monetary and economic resources from OECD countries (located on the left-side in bright colors) to recipient countries (located on the right-side in greyscale).

The default view of this visualization is set to 2016 and intentionally omits the United States to better display the contributions of all other nations. However, we invite you to adjust the temporal aspect of this diagram using the slider on the bottom of the view to the year 1960 and reintroduce the United States to the diagram using the filter option in the bottom-left corner of the visualization. We can see that in 1960 United States provided 69.3% of the total aid sources, and the primary recipients were India (36.2%) and Pakistan (12.5%). This surge was likely due to the fact that both nations were facing political disputes and economic issues during that time. Generally, it is important to note that we express these donations in terms of proportions as this scales the data and fails to acknowledge the fact that the total amount of foreign aid has dramatically increased from 1960 to 2016.

Using a Sankey diagram also introduces a different perspective to this data set as it highlights how different the donors and recipients are on a geopolitical spectrum. More over, the Sankey diagram emphasizes how foreign aid is largely a reactionary measure in the international community as clearly seen in the increase of aid economic resources send to Afghanistan, and more broadly the Middle-East, following the terrorist attacks of 2001. As seen in shifting the temporal slider bar, we clearly see a trend of developed countries providing foreign aid as a way to deploy their soft power to avoid political destabilization, yet only in response to catastrophic events in these developing nations.

Using the time filter, we can see two other significant changes compared with 1960. First, the United States now provides 33.1% of total aid, which is a considerable reduction in their participation, and countries such as United Kingdom (12%), Japan (13.8%), and Germany (11.9%) appeared as relevant contributors in 2016. Also, we can see that while there are many more recipient countries in 2016 relative to 1960, none of them receives more than Afghanistan, which was given 7% of the total aid. As with our previous visualization, we encourage you to explore the Sankey diagram in more detail by filtering by year and by different donor nations.

Concluding Thoughts

Foreign aid plays an integral role in the economies of developing nations, yet as our analysis shows, this role has fundamentally changed over time. While historically the United States and United Kingdom played outsized roles in terms of the dollar amount of their foreign aid, the landscape for donating nations in 2016 is distinctly different with the European Union collectively contributing more resources to this endeavor than the United States. Our visualizations highlight the fact that although in some cases foreign aid remain constant between nations regardless of the geopolitical climate of that time, foreign aid predominately goes to nations retroactively as a response to some political crisis, economic calamity, or severe humanitarian crisis. Key examples of these shifts in foreign aid resources displayed in our visualizations include:

  • The gradual decline and eventual fall of the Soviet Union in the late 1980’s and early 1990’s
  • The steady stream of fiscal resources towards Vietnam in the late 1960’s and early 1970’s as a result of the U.S.-led coalition during the Vietnam War
  • The impact of the 9/11 attacks and reactionary surge of resources towards the Middle-East and high-risk African nations

Somewhat masked in our visualizations, however, are the equally important success stories. For instance, India received a large bulk of foreign aid in the early 1960’s as it transitioned from colonial rule to a sovereign nation, which likely helped fuel its rapid ascent to a global economic power. It is somewhat ironic that our visualization fail to easily identify these clear benefits to foreign aid as they are incidentally the aspects so often overlooked by politicians and decision-makers aiming to save a few funds by redirecting these resources to fuel local efforts. International aid plays a critical role in reshaping global economic inequality and righting wrongs committed over centuries of colonial rule and discriminatory subjugation. Perhaps only after drawing attention to the critical nature of these issues by identifying those nations which benefited immensely from these foreign sources of assistance will international leaders both domestically and abroad truly recognize why continued financial commitment in these activities is essential for the security of our world.
